Over three decades, my work has moved between two poles I regard as inseparable: building AI systems that operate in the real world and advancing the scientific foundations that will define what those systems can become.

That duality is deliberate. Research without industrial grounding produces solutions for problems that do not exist. Industry without rigorous science produces systems that cannot be trusted, explained, or safely extended. My career has been an attempt to work at that boundary — as principal investigator on programmes funded at the scale of tens of millions of euros, as a technical lead embedded in global enterprises, and as a scientist whose work is cited by researchers across twenty countries.

I hold the Cátedra (Full Proffesorship) — the highest professorial rank in the Spanish university system — currently at CUNEF Universidad, Madrid. Simultaneously ranked in the Stanford top 2% of scientists globally, with an h-index of 39 and over 6,800 citations. My research spans explainable AI, federated learning, quantum-classical hybrid architectures, and causal machine learning, published consistently in the leading venues of each field.

The questions I find most consequential sit at the intersection of scientific rigour and organisational accountability: when can an AI system's decision be trusted? How do we train models when data cannot move? What does a genuine quantum advantage look like in a production environment?
